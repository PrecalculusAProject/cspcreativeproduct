IB Group 4 CSP – Interdisciplinary Project

Exploring How School Design Affects Human-Environment Interactions

Statement of Inquiry

To what extent does the design of North Atlanta High School contribute to positive interactions between human society and the environment?

Interdisciplinary Connection

This project brings together insights from Physics, Biology, and Sports & Exercise Health Science (SEHS). Each discipline conducted an experiment that contributes to a unified understanding of how the physical design of our school environment affects student well-being, behavior, and environmental interaction.

Physics

Research Question: How does the layout and surface type of school hallways affect students' walking speed and acceleration?

Variables: Independent – surface type; Dependent – speed/acceleration; Controlled – distance, time of day, participants

Method: Students walked 10m on tile, carpet, and concrete surfaces. Times were recorded and speeds/accelerations calculated.

SurfaceAvg. Time (s)Speed (m/s)Acceleration (m/s²)
Tile6.11.640.27
Carpet6.91.450.21
Concrete5.71.750.31

Conclusion: Surface type influences student speed, which impacts movement flow and energy use in the school environment.

Biology

Research Question: How does noise pollution in different school zones affect students' physiological stress responses?

Variables: Independent – location; Dependent – heart rate; Controlled – same individuals, time of day

Method: Students measured heart rates in quiet (library) and noisy (cafeteria, hallway) areas using fitness trackers. Noise levels were recorded with a decibel meter app.

LocationNoise Level (dB)Avg. Heart Rate (bpm)
Library4268
Hallway7478
Cafeteria8584

Conclusion: Louder environments are associated with higher heart rates, suggesting a stress response linked to school design.

Sports & Exercise Health Science (SEHS)

Research Question: How does air quality in different school areas impact cardiovascular performance during exercise?

Variables: Independent – location; Dependent – post-exercise heart rate; Controlled – same exercise, same participants

Method: Students performed light cardio in the gym, classroom, and outside. Air quality (CO₂/PM2.5) was measured, and heart rate was recorded post-exercise.

LocationCO₂ (ppm)Post-Exercise Heart Rate (bpm)
Gym850142
Classroom1100152
Outside430138

Conclusion: Better air quality correlates with lower heart rates post-exercise, suggesting outdoor areas are more conducive to healthy physical activity.

Challenges & Suggestions

  • Controlling time of day and hallway traffic was difficult. Next time, block off testing areas.
  • Biology participants had noisy surroundings; suggest using noise-canceling equipment for more accurate readings.
  • Air quality sensors had slight lag; allow time for readings to stabilize in future.

Conclusion

The design of school environments—including surface materials, acoustic levels, and air quality—clearly influences physical, physiological, and behavioral outcomes in students. Each subject demonstrated how design decisions shape human-environment interactions. This webpage integrates our findings into an accessible, creative digital product aligned with the inquiry.
